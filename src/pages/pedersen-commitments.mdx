# Pedersen commitments

A Pedersen commitment is a commitment scheme (see [what a commitment scheme
is](/what-is-a-commitment-scheme) if you haven't read that yet) built directly out
of group arithmetic instead of a hash function. That choice buys two things a hash
commitment can't give you: **perfect hiding**, and a **homomorphic property** that
lets you add committed values without opening them.

It's named for Torben P. Pedersen, who introduced the construction in *"Non-
Interactive and Information-Theoretic Secure Verifiable Secret Sharing,"* presented
at CRYPTO '91 (Santa Barbara, August 11–15, 1991), published in Lecture Notes in
Computer Science vol. 576, pp. 129–140 (Springer). In that paper the commitment
wasn't the end goal — it was one piece of a verifiable secret-sharing protocol,
where a dealer splits a secret into shares such that each participant can check
their own share is consistent without talking to anyone else, and any *k* of *n*
participants can later reconstruct the secret while fewer than *k* learn nothing
about it at all, in the strict Shannon-information sense. The commitment
construction inside that protocol turned out to be useful on its own, and today
it's usually studied and deployed independently of the secret-sharing context it
was born in. A History page placing this in the broader ZK timeline is coming in a
later phase of this site.

## The construction

You need a group in which the **discrete logarithm problem** is believed to be
hard: given two elements $g$ and $h$ of the group, finding the exponent $x$ such
that $h = g^x$ should be computationally infeasible. Two versions of this are in
common use.

### Classic version (multiplicative group mod p, as in the 1991 paper)

- $p$ is a large prime, $q$ a large prime dividing $p - 1$.
- $g$ and $h$ are generators of the subgroup of order $q$ inside $\mathbb{Z}_p^*$.
- Critically, **nobody knows $\log_g h$** — the exponent $x$ with $h = g^x$. Both
  parties can trust this if $g$ and $h$ were generated by a public, nothing-up-my-
  sleeve procedure (for example, hashing a fixed string into the group) rather than
  chosen by anyone who might have picked $h = g^x$ for a known $x$.

To commit to a message $m \in \mathbb{Z}_q$:

$$
\text{Commit}(m, r) = g^m \, h^r \bmod p, \qquad r \xleftarrow{\$} \mathbb{Z}_q
$$

To open, reveal $(m, r)$; the verifier recomputes $g^m h^r \bmod p$ and checks it
matches the commitment $C$. The live demo on the [homepage](/) runs exactly this
formula, over a 2048-bit safe-prime group.

### Modern version (elliptic curve)

The same idea, written additively in an elliptic-curve group instead of a
multiplicative group mod $p$ — this is the form you'll see in most current
cryptographic protocols and libraries:

$$
\text{Commit}(m, r) = m G + r H
$$

where $G$ is the curve's standard base point, and $H$ is a second point with no
known discrete log relative to $G$ — again produced via a public hash-to-curve
procedure rather than picked out of a hat.

## Why hiding is information-theoretic

This is the property that makes Pedersen commitments strictly stronger than a hash
commitment, and the argument is short: $r$ is drawn **uniformly at random** from
$\mathbb{Z}_q$, and $h$ generates the entire order-$q$ subgroup. That means $h^r$ is
itself uniformly distributed over that whole subgroup — every element is equally
likely. Multiplying by any fixed $g^m$ is just a relabeling (a bijection on the
subgroup), so $C = g^m h^r$ is **exactly uniformly distributed over the subgroup,
for every possible value of $m$.**

Two different messages $m$ and $m'$ therefore produce commitments with identical
probability distributions. No amount of computing power lets an observer
distinguish which message produced a given $C$, because $C$ genuinely carries zero
information about $m$ — not "effectively zero," but zero in the same sense a
one-time pad carries zero information about its plaintext. That's what
"information-theoretic" (or "unconditional," or "perfect") hiding means: the
guarantee holds against an attacker with unbounded computation, not just a
computationally limited one.

## Why binding is only computational

Binding is the flip side, and it can't also be unconditional — see the trade-off
discussed on the [commitment scheme page](/what-is-a-commitment-scheme). Here's
exactly what breaking it would require.

Suppose someone finds two different openings of the same commitment: $(m, r)$ and
$(m', r')$ with $m \ne m'$, both satisfying $g^m h^r \equiv g^{m'} h^{r'} \pmod p$.
Rearranging:

$$
g^{m - m'} \equiv h^{r' - r} \pmod p
$$

If $r' = r$, this forces $g^{m-m'} \equiv 1$, which (since $g$ has order $q$) means
$m \equiv m' \pmod q$ — contradicting $m \ne m'$. So $r' \ne r$, which means
$r' - r$ is invertible mod $q$, and we can solve directly for the discrete log of
$h$ base $g$:

$$
x = \log_g h = (m - m') \cdot (r' - r)^{-1} \bmod q
$$

In other words: **anyone who can break binding has just computed $\log_g h$.**
Binding is therefore exactly as hard as the discrete logarithm problem in this
group. Since $g$ and $h$ were generated with no known relation between them (see
above), this reduces to a problem believed to be hard — but that belief is a
computational assumption, not a mathematical certainty. If discrete log in this
group ever becomes tractable (a sufficiently large quantum computer, say), binding
breaks. Hiding wouldn't be affected at all.

A binding-sandbox demo — try to find such a collision by hand, to make concrete why
this isn't just "hard" in some vague sense — is coming in the next phase of this

## The homomorphic property

Multiply two commitments together and you get a valid commitment to the *sum* of
the underlying messages, with the randomness also summed:

$$
\text{Commit}(m_1, r_1) \cdot \text{Commit}(m_2, r_2)
= g^{m_1} h^{r_1} \cdot g^{m_2} h^{r_2}
= g^{m_1 + m_2} h^{r_1 + r_2}
= \text{Commit}(m_1 + m_2,\; r_1 + r_2)
$$

Nobody needs to open either commitment for this to work — it's pure group
arithmetic. This is the property confidential transactions and range proofs are
built on (an `/applications` page walking through those is coming soon): a system
can verify that committed inputs sum to committed outputs — proving no value was
created or destroyed — without ever learning what any individual amount was.
